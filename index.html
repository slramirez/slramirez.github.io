Research

My research uses game theory, statistical methods, experiments, surveys, and field work to improve understanding of negotiations, terrorism, and leaders.

How do mediators make a difference?

Political leaders often face domestic constraints that limit their abilities to negotiate. One hypothesis is that mediators who use secrecy and agenda-setting might make a difference.

I use game theory to prove how a mediator changes negotiations by "probing the bargaining space" -- proposing offers that a leader would accept, but could not offer on her own. Mediators alter strategic bargaining in ways that allow a leader to learn credible information about her adversary, while improving the prospects for agreement. My article describes how this mechanism works and the implications for democracies at war, institutions, and the way we negotiate.

Solo-authored. Mediation in the Shadow of an Audience: How Third Parties Use Secrecy and Agenda-Setting To Broker Settlements. Journal of Theoretical Politics, 2017. Published here. PDF, Appendix

Can we leverage observable attacks to reveal hidden motives?

Is Al Qaeda focused more on attacking the West, or on suppporting local campaigns of violence? To figure this out, we collected data on every affiliate in Al Qaeda's network, and constructed a classification scheme to differentiate affiliate conceptually and empirically. We used their targets and tactics to identify latent affiliate types, and track them over time.

We find evidence of a global-parochial divide. A majority of Al Qaeda's affiliates are "parochial" -- focused on local targets and tactics. Fewer than 12% can be classified as focusing on the West. Further, targets and tactics aid in identifying latent types and tracking them over time.


Ramirez, Shawn L., and Arianna Robbins. Targets and Tactics: Testing For A Duality Within Al Qaeda's Network. International Interactions, 2017. Published here. PDF, Appendix

Can we identify hierarchies in terrorist networks with minimal information?

Due to the covert nature of terrorism, a group's leadership, cells, and structure are inherently difficult to identify. We theorize that the direction of terrorist communications can be used to reveal sets of mutual influence and a network's latent hierarchical structure. In analyzing the 9/11 hijacker network and a larger network of 62 terrorists, we find that our method helps reveal hierarchies, leaders, and types of terrorist operatives.


Brams, Steven J., Hande Mutlu, and Shawn Ling Ramirez. Influence in Terrorist Networks: From Undirected to Directed Graphs. Studies in Conflict and Terrorism, 29.7 (2006): 703-718. Published here. PDF

Spatial and Temporal Patterns of Violence in the Isreali-Palestinian Conflict

When one thinks about violence in the I-P conflict, one often thinks of groups like Hamas. However, some of that violence is also perpetrated by unorganized civilians. Further, violence has excitation effects, where violence tends to beget more violence, but this might depend on who perpetrated that violence.

I examine micro-level data on attacks ranging from stone throwing to rockets reported in weekly reports. I find that significant differences in the spatial and temporal patterns of organized and unorganized violence using a multidimensional hawkes process model to account for temporal and spatial excitation effects as well as factors such as severity and weapon type.

International Diplomacy Project

I am collecting data using student-powered simulations to explore how multilateral negotiations and diplomacy works. The simulations have been run annually since 2013, and has expanded to include participants from Georgia Tech, Morehouse College, Spelman College, Agnes Scott, and Kennesaw State University.

As a research endeavor, I designed the project to use real world data, new technology to track participants (RFID tokens), and social media to track dialogue. This research takes the first step in identifying the value of face-time, secrecy, mediation, and bilateral talks in simulating diplomacy.
